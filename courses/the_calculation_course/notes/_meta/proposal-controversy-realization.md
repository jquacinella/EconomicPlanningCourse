# Proposal — New Controversy: Realization, Validation, and the Monetary Theory of Value

Status: draft for review (2026-04-30). Not yet wired into `_quarto.yml`. Lives here in `_meta/` to make clear it's a proposal, not an existing chapter.

## Why a new chapter and not an expansion of C6

Controversy 6 already covers the *mechanics* of the monetary circuit (endogenous money, SFC, Minsky-Keen, Cockshott-Dapprich). What it does not do, and arguably cannot do without becoming a 12,000-word chapter, is the *value-theoretic* move: that surplus value produced in production exists socially only once it is monetarily realized, and that the monetary circuit is therefore not a "coordination layer" sitting on top of value but the constitutive moment of value itself. That argument has its own canon (Heinrich, Bellofiore, Backhaus/Reichelt, value-form theory), its own internal disputes (is value pre-monetary substance or constituted-in-exchange?), and its own bridge function (it is the connective tissue between the value-theoretic Controversies 3 and 5 and the monetary Controversy 6/7). Wedging it into C6 buries the theoretical move under the SFC mechanics. A standalone Controversy with a small focused project, feeding into the bigger C6 capstone, gives the ideas the structural weight they deserve.

The proposal is therefore: insert a new Controversy "Realization, Validation, and the Monetary Theory of Value" at order 6, push current C6 (Money) → 7, and current C7 (Climate) → 8.

### Title

Controversy 6 — Realization, Validation, and the Monetary Theory of Value

### Description

Marx's salto mortale, Kalecki's profit equation, Graziani's circuit, and Bellofiore's macro-monetary reading — why surplus value has to be validated in money, and why that validation is the constitutive moment of value, not just its accounting register.

### Framing

The transformation problem (Controversy 3) and the Okishio theorem (Controversy 5) both treat value as a substance produced in production whose only remaining task is to be redistributed across sectors or summed across an economy. The monetary tradition that runs from Marx's own *Capital* Vol. II through Rosa Luxemburg, Kalecki, Graziani, Minsky, and the Italian and German Marx-rereaders argues that this gets the ontology backwards. Production produces commodities and a *claim* to value embodied in them; that claim only becomes social value when the commodity executes its "salto mortale" — the deadly leap from C to M' — in monetary exchange. Capitalism does not run on physical surpluses but on monetary commitments contracted before production and validated, or not, after it. Banks issue the initial finance that lets capitalists buy labor power; production extracts surplus labor; sale on the market validates the resulting commodities at a price that may or may not cover the original advance plus expected profit; firms that fail to validate are destroyed and their capital values written down. Crisis, in this reading, is not a deviation from equilibrium but the periodic enforcement of the validation requirement — capitalism *must* destroy itself periodically to clear the gap between produced value and validated value. This chapter sits between the value-theoretic controversies and the monetary-mechanics controversy because it is the argument that makes them the same controversy.

### Learning objectives

State precisely the difference between a *substance* theory of value (value produced in production, money is a measuring rod) and a *monetary* theory of value (value constituted in monetary exchange, production produces only a candidate). State Marx's salto mortale and explain why the realization problem is logically prior to the transformation problem, not a separate concern. Derive Kalecki's profit equation from a stock-flow consistent accounting matrix and explain why aggregate profits equal capitalist expenditure rather than worker non-consumption. Reconstruct Graziani's monetary circuit as a *macro-Marxian* object: initial finance, purchase of labor power, production, sale, validation or non-validation, repayment, destruction of money. Explain Bellofiore's macro-monetary reading of *Capital* and why he treats it as the natural completion of Marx rather than an importation from post-Keynesianism. State Heinrich's *Geltungstheorie des Werts* and the value-form critique of "embodied labor" readings. Connect the realization framework to crisis theory: why does this tradition predict periodic devaluation (capital destruction, default cascades, unemployment) as a structural feature, not an accident?

### Core concepts

*Salto mortale*: Marx's term in *Capital* Vol. I Ch. 3 for the leap from C to M — the moment a commodity's value claim is either realized in money or annihilated. *Realization problem*: the macro-aggregate version, raised by Marx in *Capital* Vol. II reproduction schemas and made central by Rosa Luxemburg — where does the demand to validate the surplus come from? *Kalecki's profit equation*: in the simplest closed economy with no government, aggregate profits Π = I + C_k − S_w, i.e. profits equal capitalist investment plus capitalist consumption minus worker saving; "workers spend what they earn, capitalists earn what they spend." This is the macro-realization condition, derivable directly from SFC accounting. *Graziani's circuit*: M → C(LP, MP) → P → C' → M'; banks issue M as initial finance, capitalists buy labor power and means of production, production yields C', sale yields M'; the spread M' − M is monetary profit, but only at the aggregate level via household and bank deposit dynamics — the famous *profit paradox* (where does the extra money to pay interest come from if banks only lent M?). *Bellofiore's macro-monetary Marx* (1989, 2004, 2009, 2018): explicitly fuses Graziani with Marx; treats initial bank finance as the monetary precondition of the wage relation; treats M' − M as monetarily realized surplus value; treats validation as the constitutive moment of abstract labor. *Heinrich's monetary theory of value* (*Wissenschaft vom Wert*, *Introduction to Three Volumes*): abstract labor does not exist as substance prior to exchange and then "appear" in money; it is brought into existence as a social magnitude in the act of exchange against money. Concrete labors are equated, retroactively and only in monetary validation, as fractions of total social labor. *Value-form theory* (Backhaus, Reichelt, Postone, Arthur): the form of value (money) is not an external dressing of a pre-existing substance but the only mode in which value exists at all under capitalism. *De Brunhoff* (*Marx on Money*, 1976): the canonical reconstruction of Marx's monetary theory as integral to value theory rather than a separate Vol. III topic. *Crisis as devaluation*: Mattick Sr., Clarke, Kliman, Roberts — the recurring destruction of capital values is what restores the conditions of profitable validation; the system *requires* periodic crises rather than suffering from them. *Toporowski / Steindl / Minsky bridge*: financialization as the modern displacement of the realization problem onto household debt, asset prices, and balance-sheet dynamics — the monetary circuit reorganized but not abolished.

### Resource schedule

Approximately 5 days. This chapter is theoretical; the heavy computational work stays in C7 (Money).

- Day 1: Marx, *Capital* Vol. I Ch. 3 (the money chapter) and selected passages from Vol. II Ch. 20–21 (the reproduction schemas). Free at marxists.org. Read for the salto mortale and the structure of Departments I and II. Pair with de Brunhoff, *Marx on Money* (1976), Part I — the cleanest reconstruction of Marx's own monetary theory.
- Day 2: Kalecki, *Selected Essays on the Dynamics of the Capitalist Economy* (Cambridge 1971), especially "The Determinants of Profits" (1942) and "The Principle of Increasing Risk" (1937). Short, clean, derive the profit equation by hand. Pair with Toporowski, *Michał Kalecki: An Intellectual Biography*, Vol. II (Palgrave 2018), Chapter on the profit equation, for context on its Marxian roots.
- Day 3: Graziani, *The Monetary Theory of Production* (Cambridge 2003) — already on the C7 reading list, but read here for the *theoretical* core (the profit paradox, the role of banks as third agents) rather than the mechanics.
- Day 4: Bellofiore, "A Monetary Labor Theory of Value" (*Review of Radical Political Economics* 21:1–2, 1989) — the foundational paper. Free via library. Then Bellofiore & Realfonzo, "Finance and the Labor Theory of Value: Toward a Macroeconomic Theory of Distribution from a Monetary Perspective" (*International Journal of Political Economy* 27:2, 1997). Optional advanced: Bellofiore (ed.), *Marxian Economics: A Reappraisal* Vol. 1 (Macmillan 1998), the introduction.
- Day 5: Heinrich, *An Introduction to the Three Volumes of Karl Marx's Capital* (Monthly Review 2012), Chapters 3 and 10 — the monetary theory of value and the theory of crisis. Pair with one short value-form text: either Arthur, "The Spectral Ontology of Value" (1996) or Postone, *Time, Labor, and Social Domination* (Cambridge 1993), Ch. 3 — read for the value-form critique of substance readings.
- Day 6: Project.

### Mini project — Kalecki's profit equation and a two-department circuit

Three short numerical exercises that together earn the theoretical apparatus computationally, and feed directly into the C7 capstone (Versions 1 and 2).

1. *Kalecki from accounting.* Build a 4×4 SFC transactions matrix for a closed economy with workers, capitalists, firms, and a government. Verify that every column sums to zero (budget constraints) and every row sums to zero (every flow has a counterparty). Derive Kalecki's profit equation Π = I + C_k − S_w directly from the matrix by sectoral subtraction. Run a numerical example: hold worker saving at zero, vary capitalist investment over a range, plot aggregate profits. Confirm the identity numerically. Repeat with positive worker saving and show how it subtracts from realized profits — the macro statement of the realization problem.

2. *Two-department reproduction with monetary financing.* Implement Marx's Vol. II Department I / Department II reproduction schema (means of production / wage goods) in two versions. Version A: barter — Department I sells means of production to itself and to Department II; Department II sells wage goods to workers in both departments; check the exchange ratios required for simple reproduction. Version B: monetary — a bank issues initial finance to both departments to pay wages before any sale has occurred; workers spend on Department II; Department II buys means of production from Department I; both departments repay the bank. Show that the *same* physical reproduction schema is consistent with multiple monetary realizations, and that a shortfall in initial finance (or in the velocity of repayment) breaks reproduction at the realization step even when production proceeds normally. This is the computational form of "value produced ≠ value realized."

3. *Realization failure and devaluation.* Take Version B and impose a 10% shortfall in Department II demand (workers save instead of spending). Trace the consequences: Department II cannot validate its full output, cannot fully repay the bank, must write down inventory and lay off workers; this propagates back to Department I as reduced demand for means of production. Show numerically that the system reaches a new lower-output equilibrium with destroyed capital values. Compare to the Okishio framework (Controversy 5): Okishio assumes validation is automatic, so the falling-rate-of-profit debate looks different once realization is on the table.

Write 600 words tying the three exercises back to the readings: where does Kalecki's profit equation appear in Bellofiore? Where does Heinrich's "value is constituted in exchange" show up in your Version B? What does the realization-failure exercise commit you to about crisis theory — does capitalism *need* periodic devaluation, or merely tolerate it? And finally: in the Cockshott-Cottrell-Dapprich planned economy (which you'll build in C7), what *replaces* the validation step? Is there a planned-economy analog of the salto mortale, or does the whole problem dissolve?

### Self-check

State the salto mortale in one sentence. Why is the realization problem logically prior to the transformation problem? Derive Kalecki's profit equation from a 4×4 SFC matrix. State Graziani's profit paradox and explain why it is a *theoretical* problem about the closure of the circuit, not just an accounting curiosity. What does Bellofiore claim is the role of bank finance in *Marx's* theory, not just in post-Keynesianism? State Heinrich's monetary theory of value in one sentence and contrast it with a substance reading. In a two-department reproduction schema, can simple reproduction proceed if monetary financing is insufficient even when physical proportions are correct? Why does this tradition predict periodic devaluation as structural, not accidental?

### Bridge

You now have the theoretical claim that money is the constitutive moment of value, not its measuring rod, and that the validation requirement structurally produces crises. Controversy 7 is where you build the *machinery* — endogenous money, stock-flow consistent macro models, Minsky-Keen debt dynamics — and then show how the Cockshott-Cottrell-Dapprich apparatus dissolves the validation problem by separating the social-allocation function from the value-validation function. The capstone project there extends the two-department exercise from this chapter to four full versions, ending in a planned-economy comparison.
