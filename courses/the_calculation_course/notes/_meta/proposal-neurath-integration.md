# Proposal — Integrating Otto Neurath into the Calculation Course

Status: draft for review (2026-05-01). Plan-only document. No chapter files have been edited; nothing is wired into `_quarto.yml`. Lives here in `_meta/` to make clear it is a proposal, not an existing change.

## Why this proposal exists

Otto Neurath (1882–1945) is the missing origin of the calculation debate. Mises's 1920 essay "Economic Calculation in the Socialist Commonwealth" — the opener of Controversy 2 as currently written — is *literally a reply to Neurath*. Its named target is Neurath's 1919 *Durch die Kriegswirtschaft zur Naturalwirtschaft* and the Bavarian socialization scheme Neurath helped draft as head of the Munich Soviet Republic's Central Economic Office. The current C2 framing inherits the standard Austrian retelling that treats Mises as the opener and Lange/Hayek/Cockshott as successive replies; this is historically inverted, and a reader who knows the period will spot the omission immediately. Neurath also matters technically: his *Naturalrechnung* (calculation in kind) is the position that planning can proceed in physical units — kWh, tons, hectares, hours — without reduction to a common scalar. Cockshott/Cottrell's labor-time approach is, viewed through Neurath, a *partial retreat* from the original program: they accept one common scalar (labor hours) where Neurath rejected reduction outright. And the dual labor-/CO₂- accounting in Controversy 8 is structurally a Neurathian move whose lineage runs through Martinez-Alier and O'Neill back to Neurath himself, but the chapter never names the tradition. The Cosmonaut critique's complaint that "the relationship between labor-time and CO₂ as parallel accounting units needs more theoretical work than it receives" is, in effect, a request for the Neurath-O'Neill apparatus.

## Recommended approach: hybrid weave, not a new standalone controversy

The realization-controversy proposal already pushes the count from seven to eight controversies (plus the Chinese-school seminar). Adding a standalone Neurath controversy would push to nine, which competes structurally with Part I's ten-week arc and dilutes the existing tight sequence. More substantively: Neurath's contribution is *best illustrated inside the existing debates*. Sitting him next to Mises in C2 is the historically correct move; making the implicit Neurath of W8 and C8 explicit is the technically correct move. The proposal is therefore a four-touchpoint weave — three modifications to existing chapters plus one optional new appendix — and not a new chapter.

Alternative considered and rejected: standalone "Controversy — Calculation in Kind". Rejected because it would (a) push the controversy count to a structurally awkward nine, (b) repeat material that more naturally lives in C2's framing and C8's lineage, and (c) detach Neurath from the conversations he was actually part of. Alternative also considered: a single substantial expansion of C2 only. Rejected because it leaves the W8 in-natura claim under-developed and the C8 multi-unit accounting un-grounded.

## Touch point 1 — Controversy 2 (Socialist Calculation Debate)

Restore Neurath as the chapter's first voice rather than its erased pre-history. Concretely:

*Framing.* Rewrite the opening sentence so the debate begins in 1919 with Neurath, not in 1920 with Mises. New text along these lines: "This debate opens in 1919, not 1920. The position Mises is attacking in his famous essay is Otto Neurath's argument — published as *Durch die Kriegswirtschaft zur Naturalwirtschaft* and elaborated in the Bavarian and Saxon socialization schemes Neurath helped draft — that an economy can be coordinated in physical units (kWh, tons, hectares, hours) without reduction to a common monetary scalar. Mises's reply names Neurath explicitly and argues that *Naturalrechnung* cannot rationally compare alternative production plans because it lacks a common unit of account."

*Learning objectives.* Add: "You can state Neurath's *Naturalrechnung* position and distinguish it from later monist responses (Lange's price-equilibrium monism, Cockshott's labor-time monism). You can explain why the Mises argument is most directly an attack on Neurath, and why Hayek's 1945 reformulation in informational terms is partly a *concession* that Neurath's specific position about non-monetary calculation cannot be refuted on purely calculational grounds."

*Core concepts.* Add a paragraph after the existing Mises paragraph: "Neurath's *Naturalrechnung* (calculation in kind): planning is a problem of choosing between non-commensurable life-situations (*Lebenslagen*), each described as a vector of physical magnitudes; rational comparison is possible without scalar reduction, via what we would now call multi-criteria or Pareto-ranked decision procedures. The wartime German planning experience (the Kriegsrohstoffabteilung, the Reichsamt für die Volksernährung) is Neurath's empirical proof-of-concept; he treats WWI as having demonstrated that an industrial economy can be coordinated in tons-of-coal and bushels-of-grain rather than in marks. Polanyi's 1922 *socialist accounting* response is a partial third position — accepting Mises's critique of pure in-kind calculation but proposing a two-level scheme (associations bargaining over physical quantities, with monetary calculation only at the surface). Weber's 1921/22 *Wirtschaft und Gesellschaft* §12–14 contains a more sympathetic but still skeptical engagement with Neurath."

*Resource schedule.* Insert as a new Day 1 (push current Day 1 Mises to Day 2, etc.; budget remains 7 days because we trim one of the secondary-source days):

- Day 1 (NEW): Neurath, primary sources. Read "Total Socialization" (1920) and the relevant chapters of *Economic Plan and Calculation in Kind* (1925). Both are available in English in *Otto Neurath: Economic Writings 1904–1945*, ed. Thomas Uebel & Robert Cohen (Kluwer 2004) — university library; ~$60 used as a hardcover. Free online alternative: O'Neill's *The Market: Ethics, Knowledge, and Politics* (Routledge 1998), Chapter 8, which excerpts and reconstructs the core argument with citations. For context, read Uebel, "Calculation in Kind and Marketless Socialism: On Otto Neurath's Utopian Economics" (*European Journal of the History of Economic Thought*, 2005), free PDF on the author's page.

The existing Day 1 Mises becomes Day 2 and gains a sentence: "Read Mises specifically as a *reply* to yesterday's Neurath. Notice the rhetorical structure: Mises is not introducing a new problem but trying to close down a position that already had institutional momentum."

The remaining days slide; trim one day from the existing Day 5 "state of the debate" load (move Boettke-Candela to "further reading") so the total stays at seven study days plus project.

*Mini project.* Add a "Version C — Neurathian multi-unit plan" alongside the existing Version A (Cockshott LP) and Version B (Lange tâtonnement). Reuse the same five-sector economy. Instead of a single scalar objective, define three resource constraints — labor hours, energy units (kWh), and a critical material (say, steel-equivalent). Solve as a *vector* optimization problem: rather than collapsing to one objective, return the Pareto frontier in (consumption-scale, energy-use, material-use) space using either the ε-constraint method or weighted-sum scalarization with a swept weight grid. Plot the frontier. Show explicitly that the LP's *labor-time* dual price (Version A) is one specific scalarization point on this frontier, corresponding to the choice "label labor as the only binding constraint." Discussion question: when the planner faces a frontier rather than a point, what does the *political* choice between frontier points look like, and is it the same kind of choice that Hayek argued markets are uniquely good at? This is the chapter's most direct Neurath payoff — it makes visible that scalar reduction is a choice, not a logical necessity.

*Self-check.* Add: "State Neurath's *Naturalrechnung* position in one sentence. Why does the Mises argument bite harder against Neurath than against Lange? Why does it bite harder against Lange than against Cockshott? In your Version C Pareto frontier, what does it mean to say 'no single optimum exists' — is that a defect of the formulation or its main virtue? What kind of decision procedure would you need on top of the frontier to actually choose a plan, and is that procedure economic, political, or something else?"

*Bridge.* No change needed; the existing bridge to C3 (transformation problem) still works.

## Touch point 2 — Week 8 (Planning and Allocation)

W8 currently mentions "Marginal-substitution-free planning 'in natura' (Cockshott)" parenthetically in core concepts. Expand and disambiguate.

*Core concepts.* Replace the parenthetical with: "Planning *in natura* in two distinct senses. (i) Neurath's full *Naturalrechnung*: no scalar reduction; the plan is a vector of physical quantities chosen against multiple physical constraints, and trade-offs between non-commensurable goods (labor-time vs energy vs land vs emissions) are resolved by political/deliberative procedure rather than by reduction to a single numéraire. (ii) Cockshott's labor-time-monist 'in natura': scalar reduction to one number — labor hours — but no monetary prices. The two are distinct positions; Cockshott is sometimes described as a Neurathian but the labor-time monism is a real concession to Mises that Neurath himself never made."

*Learning objectives.* Add: "Distinguish full *Naturalrechnung* from labor-time-monist planning. Recognize that the multi-resource LP variant of the planning problem (the energy-constraint extension in the mini-project) is technically a Neurathian formulation: each additional resource constraint adds a dimension to the dual-price vector, and the planner chooses an objective only by selecting which dimensions to scalarize."

*Resource schedule.* Add a half-day reading insertion in Day 2 or Day 3: O'Neill, "In Partial Praise of a Positivist: The Work of Otto Neurath" (*Radical Philosophy* 74, 1995), free online; this is a 12-page article that reconstructs Neurath specifically as a planning theorist and is the best single secondary source for the connection to modern multi-resource LP. Optional supplement: Martinez-Alier, *Ecological Economics: Energy, Environment and Society* (Blackwell 1987), Chapter 11 ("Otto Neurath and the Calculation Debates").

*Mini project.* The existing project already supports a multi-resource constraint (the energy variant in the second sub-task). Reframe its description so the Neurath connection is explicit: "When you add the energy constraint, you have written down a *Naturalrechnung* — two resource dimensions, no scalar reduction at the constraint level. The dual variables are now a 2-vector of shadow prices: hours-per-unit and kWh-per-unit. There is no logical move that collapses these into one number; any such collapse is an additional choice (an assumed exchange rate, a political weighting, a market price). Note this. Most of the rest of the controversy literature *implicitly* makes that additional choice without naming it as a choice."

*Self-check.* Add: "If your planning LP has three resource constraints, you have three shadow prices. What logical operation would let you collapse them into one number, and is that operation available within the LP itself? If not, what kind of operation is it?"

## Touch point 3 — Controversy 8 (Climate-Constrained Planning)

C8 already does the technical work of dual labor-/CO₂-accounting. What it lacks is the lineage that explains *why* this works and why the Cosmonaut critique's worry — "the labor-time-plus-CO₂ apparatus needs more theoretical work" — is solvable rather than terminal. The Neurath-O'Neill ecological-economics tradition is exactly that theoretical work.

*Framing.* Add a paragraph: "The dual accounting framework — every commodity priced in both labor-time and embodied CO₂ — is not a Cockshott innovation. It is the contemporary form of an argument that runs from Neurath's *Naturalrechnung* (1919–1925) through Kapp (1950) and Martinez-Alier (1987) to John O'Neill's 1990s revival in ecological economics. The claim throughout is that an industrial-ecological economy is intrinsically multi-dimensional in its accounting, and that monetary or labor-time monism is an analytical choice imposed on the data, not a feature of the economy itself. The Cosmonaut review's complaint that the labor-CO₂ duality 'needs more theoretical work' is, read against this tradition, a request to make the Neurathian commitment explicit and own its consequences."

*Core concepts.* Add: "Embodied-CO₂ accounting via parallel Leontief inverse is structurally identical to embodied-labor accounting; the two are independent runs of the same computation against different coefficient vectors. The Neurath-O'Neill move is to refuse to combine them: keep both vectors, present trade-offs explicitly, decide via political deliberation which constraint binds when. This is what gives the dual-accounting framework its theoretical legs against the Cosmonaut critique."

*Resource schedule.* Add a half-day to the existing Day 3 (Roberts) or Day 4 (Cosmonaut): O'Neill, *Markets, Deliberation and Environment* (Routledge 2007), Chapter 4, on Neurath as a planning theorist for the ecological era. Free chapters are available on the author's institutional page. Alternative shorter reading: O'Neill, "Socialist Calculation and Environmental Valuation: Money, Markets and Ecology" (*Science & Society* 66:1, 2002), free via JSTOR.

*Mini project.* Reframe Part E.2 (Cosmonaut critique stress test) so it directly engages Neurath. Current text: "the labor-plus-CO₂ dual accounting — does it actually produce coherent shadow prices, or are the two constraints pulling in ways that create internal contradictions?" Replace with: "the labor-plus-CO₂ dual accounting in the Neurathian reading is *not supposed to* collapse to a coherent single shadow price — that is the point. Run your Part C plan with the emissions constraint binding, then with the labor constraint binding, then with both binding, and report what changes about the optimal sectoral allocation. The 'incoherence' Cosmonaut points to is the Neurathian feature: the plan presents the planner with a choice frontier rather than a single answer. Discuss whether this is a strength or a weakness of the framework, in conversation with O'Neill 1995/2002."

*Self-check.* Add: "Why is the dual labor-/CO₂-accounting framework Neurathian rather than Cockshottian? What does the Neurath-O'Neill tradition say about why we should *not* try to collapse the dual prices to a single number? In one sentence, how would you reply to the Cosmonaut review's worry that the duality is theoretically underdeveloped?"

*Bridge.* No change needed.

## Touch point 4 (optional) — New Appendix D: Calculation in Kind

A standalone deeper treatment for readers who want the full apparatus. Modeled on the existing extra-depth appendix B; sits behind the main spine and is referenced from C2, W8, and C8 rather than being on the critical path. Skip if the author wants minimum disruption — the three weave touch points above carry the substantive load. Include if the author wants Neurath to have a single self-contained reference treatment in the book.

Draft outline:

1. **Historical context.** Munich Soviet Republic 1919; Neurath as head of the Central Economic Office; the Bavarian socialization scheme; Red Vienna municipal socialism; the Vienna Circle / *Wiener Kreis* and Neurath's parallel work on logical empiricism and Isotype (visual statistics for planning).
2. **The technical core.** *Naturalrechnung* formalized as multi-criteria optimization. Lebenslagen as vectors in a high-dimensional commodity-and-quality space. Comparison without scalar reduction: lexicographic ordering, Pareto-dominance, ε-constraint, weighted-sum scalarization with explicit political weighting. The relationship to modern goal programming (Charnes-Cooper-Ferguson 1955) and vector optimization (Edgeworth → Pareto → modern multi-objective LP).
3. **The Mises 1920 reply, in Neurath's terms.** What Mises is and is not refuting. Why Lange and Cockshott both *concede* something to Mises that Neurath never did. Why Hayek 1945 partly retreats to information-economics ground because the calculational ground is harder to defend against Neurath than the Austrian narrative typically admits.
4. **The ecological-economics revival.** Kapp 1950; Martinez-Alier 1987; O'Neill 1995 / 1998 / 2002 / 2007; Spash on incommensurability; recent Saito and Vettese-Pendergrass uses. Why the climate transition is the canonical contemporary case for *Naturalrechnung*.
5. **A worked example.** A small Neurathian plan for a three-sector / three-resource economy. Pareto frontier in 3-D, with a JSXGraph or Plotly interactive that lets the reader sweep the scalarization weights and watch the optimum slide along the frontier. This is the appendix's payoff: the reader sees with their eyes that *the optimum is not unique until the political choice is made*.
6. **Open theoretical questions.** The aggregation problem (when does a Pareto frontier reduce to a workable handful of choices?). The deliberation problem (what political procedure picks a frontier point, and is it economic theory's job to specify it?). The information problem (does Neurathian planning escape Hayek 1945 or just relocate the difficulty?). Honest treatment, not a victory lap.

If included, the appendix is the natural home for the Vienna Circle / Isotype context that doesn't belong on the main spine but rewards the reader who chases it.

## Open questions for the author

1. **Hybrid weave vs standalone Controversy — confirm.** The proposal recommends weaving Neurath into C2/W8/C8 plus an optional Appendix D, rather than adding a ninth standalone controversy. The argument is structural (8 + Neurath as standalone = 9, awkward against Part I's 10-week arc) and substantive (Neurath belongs *inside* the existing debates, not adjacent to them). Is this the right call? The standalone option is also defensible if you want Neurath to have his own chapter the way Sraffa and Okishio do.
2. **Appendix D — include or skip?** It is the cleanest home for the Vienna Circle / Isotype / Lebenslagen material that doesn't fit on the main spine. But it adds ~3,000 words of optional content. Skip for v1 and add later if reader demand justifies it?
3. **Reading list cost.** The Uebel & Cohen *Economic Writings* hardcover is $60+ used; there is no free legal full-text. The proposal currently uses O'Neill's reconstructions and free secondary sources as the workable substitute, with the primary source as "if you have access." Acceptable, or should the chapter pre-suppose the primary source?
4. **C2 mini-project Version C scope.** Adding a Pareto-frontier sub-project to C2 makes the chapter heavier (the 7-day budget is already on the longer side). Options: (a) keep at 7 days and trim Boettke-Candela as planned; (b) extend to 8 days and own the heaviness; (c) make Version C a "stretch goal" and not part of the required project. Preference?
5. **Polanyi 1922 — include?** Karl Polanyi's "Sozialistische Rechnungslegung" (*Archiv für Sozialwissenschaft und Sozialpolitik* 49, 1922) is the natural third interlocutor of the 1919–1922 Vienna debate and a useful counterweight to both Neurath and Mises. It is also a hard read in the original German with no clean English translation in print. Include as further reading only, or skip?
6. **Weber's response.** *Wirtschaft und Gesellschaft* §12–14 contains Weber's measured-skeptical engagement with Neurath. This is canonical for a Vienna-debate seminar treatment but probably too heavy for a Day 1 reading. Mention in passing and link to Appendix D, or skip entirely?
7. **Connection to the realization-controversy proposal.** Both proposals make the same structural move: they argue that the existing chapter sequence misses a constitutive theoretical voice (Bellofiore/Heinrich for monetary value; Neurath for in-kind calculation). Is there a unifying preface paragraph for Part II that names this pattern, or is it cleaner to leave each chapter to make its own historical-recovery argument?
8. **Isotype.** Neurath's visual-statistics work (Isotype, the Vienna Method of Pictorial Statistics) has a real claim on a course about computational economic communication. Off-topic for the math spine, but plausibly on-topic for a future "communicating planning" appendix or the post-course reading ladder. Park it, or note it now?
